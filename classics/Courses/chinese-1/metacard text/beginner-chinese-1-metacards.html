Pinyin Tones

Chinese is a language that uses different tone inflections to distinguish between similar characters. Every character has a tone and there are a total of five distinct tones. When the characters are represented in Pinyin, which is the standard way to depict the character phonetically, the tone is written with line(s) on top of the word.

ā á ǎ à

Pinyin Alphabet

Now that you learned about the five different tones in Chinese, the next step is to combine the tones with the actual pronunciation of the characters. Certain consonant and vowel sounds are different than they are pronounced in English. For example, the 'q' sound is said with a 'ch' sound rather than a 'k' sound in Chinese.

Character Radicals

Even though you can type Chinese on a keyboard using Pinyin, writing Chinese is a different story. A Chinese radical is a graphical component of a Chinese character. Together, the radicals make up the written structure of the character, and often give it semantic or phonetic meaning. The shape and position of these radicals are also important.

亻 扌 也 ⽑ ⼏

Sentence formation

Subject + 是 + object

There are no conjugations in Chinese and sentences follow a pretty simple grammatical structure. In the present tense, to say that 'A is B,' you use the character for 'is' which is 是. However, when you are using an adjective instead of an object to describe the subject, the 是 is left out in the middle of the sentence.

  • tā shì nán hái

    他 + 是 + 男孩。

    He is a boy.

  • tā mén hěn máng

    他们 + 很忙。

    They are very busy.

Sentence formation

Subject + 在 + gerund/place

In the present tense, when you want to say that 'A is doing B,' you use 在. The verb that follows would be treated as a gerund in English. Since 在 also literally means 'is at' in Chinese, you can also say that 'A is at B,' given that B is a location.

  • wǒ zài chī píng guǒ

    我 + 在 + 吃苹果。

    I am eating an apple.

  • wǒ mén zài tú shū guǎn

    我们 + 在 + 图书馆。

    We are at the library.

Sentence formation

Subject + verb + object

The general structure when a subject does something follows a simple pattern. However, the main verb oftentimes follows a helping verb, such as 在, when you are describing a subject doing a single action in the present. When the subject frequently does the action over a longer period of time, you eliminate the use of 在.

  • wǒ yào píng guǒ

    我 + 要 + 苹果。

    I want an apple.

  • wǒ mén bú zài chī yú

    我们 + 不在 + 吃鱼。

    We are not eating fish.

  • wǒ mén bù chī yú

    我们 + 不 + 吃鱼。

    We do not eat fish.

Sentence formation

Subject + 不 + Verb + Object

It is time to learn negation! When you are saying 'A is not B,' use the character 不 for not. It can also be used in front of an adjective to describe the semantic opposite of that adjective.

  • wǒ bú shì dì dì

    我 + 不是 + 弟弟。

    I am not the younger brother.

  • tā mén bú zài wán yóu xì

    他们 + 不在 + 玩游戏。

    We are not playing games.

  • wǒ bù cōng míng

    我 + 不 + 聪明。

    I am not smart.

Sentence formation

Subject + 没有 + Noun/Verb

Another way to express negation is with 没, which roughly translates to meaning 'none.' When used with 有, meaning 'to have.' it means 'to not have.' Thus, when you say 'A does not have B,' you use the characters 没有.

  • wǒ méi yǒu jiě jiě

    我 + 没有 + 姐姐。

    I do not have an older sister.

  • wǒ mén méi yǒu shí jiān le

    我们 + 没有 + 时间了。

    We do not have time.
